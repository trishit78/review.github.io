By Trishit Bhowmik

I can't remember when I first saw Tom Cruise, but it was probably on my computer. I'm not sure whose movie it was, but it was definitely a torrent. I grew to love him over time because of the life-or-death stunts he does for us on TV. He is, as far as I'm aware that he is the last great movie star to live.

Cruise’s reputation has ranged over the years from hero to villain to general curiosity. At nearly 60, one might imagine he’s done. But here he is, proving everyone wrong, at the helm of a $170m action movie that broke US box office records on its opening weekend.

The 1986 original of Top Gun was cleverly capitalised on by the sequel's creators, who updated the fighter jets and crew while liberally infusing nods to the first film into every aspect of the sequel. It's been more than 35 years since the release of the original Top Gun, in which Tom Cruise employed his widest grin as a US Navy aviator with a point to prove and a childlike delight in playing with high-speed toys (which just happen to be built for killing people, but whatever).

Now talking about this film, Tom returns to the cockpit as Pete "Maverick" Mitchell, still feeling the need for speed no matter what the top brass says. And now, enough time has passed since his co-pilot Goose's death in the original film for Goose's son to be a fully grown man, who is portrayed by Miles Teller, is a flying Rooster with the Navy and is a chip off the old block.

The opening credits describe Maverick, like the original. The opening text caption explaining the concept of the US Navy's Fighter Weapons School uses the same wording as the first film. And throughout, director Joseph Kosinski and cinematographer Claudio Miranda faithfully re-create the late Tony Scott's cinematic style.

The flight deck sequence has zero connection to what comes after, but it's still a pretty great introduction, instantly immersing you in the familiar feel of a film you may have seen many times or may not have seen for years. More importantly, it feels real, the film setting out its stall from the very beginning: It's about real stuff, like fighter planes and sailboats and proper old-fashioned stunts, not fake stuff like drones and phones and computer-generated spectacle. Top Gun: Maverick uses the visual language of the original, the camera jammed claustrophobically into a cockpit or shaking as it struggles to keep up with a jet screaming past.

There's no disguising that a lot of the story is a rerun of the original. For example, Cruise takes the Kelly McGillis role, just for fun. But somehow, despite the fact it's all geared toward a life-or-death mission, the stakes don't feel as immediate as they did the first time around. The original film was fuelled by the sense Maverick was genuinely dangerous to the people around him, but this new model doesn't capture the same headlong rush into the danger zone. Partly because the younger models look more like, well, models, rather than warriors. But the main problem is that the mission is so improbably specific to the needs of the plot. The G-force of narrative silliness will start to crush your brain, especially when a late-stage twist fires the afterburners and jets into absurdity that might tempt you to eject.

You can get all of this all of that regret in the relationship Maverick has with Penny Benjamin, and how this is the way he wants it to be. He's going to make up for it. But remember, the real love story has always been between Maverick and Iceman. And that's where the beating heart of the film strikes the strongest Val Kilmer’s battle with cancer, which he's managed to win thankfully, a year since the movie was filmed is integrated into the story, which is one of the most tasteful uses of an actor who is transformed so significantly since the last time we saw them in this property. Iceman only gets one scene in the movie, and it's heart-breaking because of the unavoidable real-life parallels, but it's also powerful, Maverick comes to Iceman not knowing what to do with the rooster and his assignment and Iceman, who can barely speak and largely communicates through typing now do not waste time or mince words. He's jovial towards his run, but also blunt. Maverick isn't struggling with a rooster he's struggling with a goose. He's struggling with his past demons, that he had to let go of. Maverick has to let Rooster be his own man. But the reality is that Maverick has been holding on to the goose since he was cradling his body in the water. He let go physically, but he hasn't let go. It's holding him back from giving his students what they need from giving Rooster what he needs, inside and outside of the cockpit and from giving himself what he needs. Iceman drills this into Maverick's head and doesn't give him an inch to shy away from the lesson that he needs to learn from the fact that even the world's best pilot doesn't know everything. This is an incredible scene.

Top Gun: Maverick, meanwhile, doesn't even tell us who Tom's fighting against. There's an unnamed faceless adversary, black-helmeted bogeys and boogeymen, stripped of sovereignty or even humanity. The eternal enemy, somewhere out there, doing vaguely defined bad-sounding things that need to be blown up by missiles and helicopters and aircraft carriers.

But who cares about that? This isn't Saving Private Ryan, this is Top Gun. Anyone who loves a great popcorn action movie that's as enjoyable as it is ridiculous. Top Gun: Maverick is a blast. The film keeps insisting this is Maverick's last post, but this polished action movie powerhouse is a fun way to fly into the sunset.
